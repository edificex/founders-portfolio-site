Motivation: Why I want to do the course

To Learn

The main reason I am keen to do the Founders & Coders course this year, is to learn! I have been fiddling at the edges of the technological sphere for a long time, but have never moved past the point of keen amateur. What that has looked like thus far has mostly been motivated by political priority and sheer curiosity. For example, I have learnt a lot of obscure details about phone security, what tools we can use to communicate in an end-to-end encrypted fashion (e.g. Signal), and how to combat the various tech-monopolies that hoover up your data on the daily.

What I have lacked at every stage though, is any kind of technical grounding. This is why I have been wanting to do a development bootcamp for several years now. I have umpteen bizarre ideas kicking around inside my head, and I can't tell which of them are feasible as individual projects, which would take a huge team years to produce, and which are just not going to happen. I want some more tools with which to bring my rogue imagination, and those of the people around me, to life. And judging by how much I have enjoyed working through the pre-requisites, I would love every minute of it.

To Co-Operate

Founders stuck out to me as the course to apply to because it seemed genuinely geared towards doing technological work for socially constructive ends. I also really like how it is organised - that everyone who does the course helps reproduce it for future coders. I have (somewhat trepidatiously) already started thinking about how much fun it would be to put my workshop planning and faciliation skills to use with cohorts down the line.

Most importantly, I am really excited at the prospect of meeting lots of other folks who share some of this desire to use tech 'for good', or who want to play with these systems to see what cool stuff we can make them do. I love working with other people - although I've enjoyed coding alone at the terminal this past while, it is definitely alienating, and I only get to see the problem at hand one way! It's a slow and frustrating process compared to what I know is possible with a collectivised approach.

Also, let's be honest, who can afford one of these corporate pipeline bootcamps...

To Build Tools for Struggle

I read often and avidly about advances in AI, machine learning and their applications. It's very interesting but it's also a painful process. You end up wincing constantly at how effectively capitalism is mobilising exciting new technologies for reactionary purposes. Apps that discipline courier workers for a late drop, algorithms trained on the historic data amassed by racist institutions used to decide bail conditions, the generalisation of facial recognition by law enforcement. The list goes on.

The real nub of all this is that for every horror, you can imagine a hundred emancipatory possiblities - but apparently these aren't profitable options. For example, where are the platforms that help you organise your workplace, or successfully navigate asylum claims or the hostile environment? In summary, I want to level up my skills so I can better contribute to the proliferation of these kinds of systems, and to better resist the wielding of technological innovation against 99% of people.

To make money

It remains the case that in order to pay rent and buy food I have to work. It is also true that I find coding immensely more satisfying and mentally sustainable than customer service, and would love to be able to survive (and maybe even thrive!) by finding work in this field.
